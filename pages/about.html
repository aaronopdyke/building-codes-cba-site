About this demo

A cost-benefit explorer for seismic building-code adoption and enforcement. For each country it models today's building stock on a 1 km grid (floor areas × unit construction costs × GEM building typologies × construction-vintage code levels), pairs it with GEM vulnerability functions and the GEM seismic hazard map, and runs a 2025–2075 cost-benefit analysis of raising new construction to the next code level — across all five SSP growth scenarios.

What the map shows

Everything is aggregated onto H3 hexagons (size auto-scaled to the country; roughly 36–253 km² per hex). Values are summed per hex and ratios derived after aggregation. Map views:

  • Seismic hazard — mean peak ground acceleration with a 475-year return period (GEM Global Seismic Hazard Map).
  • Exposure 2025 — building replacement value per hex.
  • Growth to 2075 — new floor area added between 2025 and 2075 (SSP2 forecast).
  • Risk metrics:
    • BCR — total benefits of all three resilience dividends ÷ discounted costs (SSP2) — the same ratio as the tiles and the chart. National program costs (and their stimulus) are allocated to hexes by their share of discounted new-construction value.
    • Total benefits — NPV of all three dividends (SSP2).
    • Annual loss — baseline 2025 average annual earthquake loss (USD/yr).
    • Loss rate — the average annual earthquake loss expressed as a share of the building stock's replacement value (e.g. 0.2% means yearly expected losses equal to 0.2% of what the buildings would cost to rebuild).

Triple dividend of resilience

Benefits are grouped under the Triple Dividend of Resilience framework (Tanner et al., ODI/GFDRR/World Bank), with each dollar counted exactly once:

  • 1st dividend — avoided losses when disasters strike: the headline avoided structural + contents losses (direct damage only), plus the embodied carbon of avoided reconstruction — carbon intensity from GEM's carbon accounting (CARBON_BUILDINGS_TON in the exposure model), valued at the World Bank shadow price of carbon. Lives saved and DALYs averted are annotated as counts.
  • 2nd dividend — unlocked economic potential: the GDP stimulus of the code program's spending (public-investment multipliers — IMF 2014 0.25/0.5, Adarov 2024/Ardanaz 2025 1.1 reference, Barbero 2024 RRF 1.22; multiplier channel only), with jobs preserved and created annotated as counts.
  • 3rd dividend — co-benefits of the investment itself: durability — code-compliant construction lasts longer, so the annualized cost of premature replacement falls on the compliant new stock (service life 50→60 years ≈ 0.33%/yr of compliant value).

There is exactly one benefit-cost ratio on this site: total benefits of all three dividends ÷ costs, identical in the summary tiles, the cumulative chart, and every hex cell on the map. DALYs are estimated from fatalities (GEM publishes no injury functions): deaths × years of life lost (WB life expectancy) plus an injury ratio — parameters stated in the notebook outputs. Context statistics cite EM-DAT (CRED/UCLouvain), Disease Control Priorities 3rd ed. (2018), and World Bank labour-force data.

Retrofit priorities

Code changes only reach new construction. The retrofit table ranks the existing stock's building classes by avoided AAL if retrofitted: engineered classes move one code level up (GEM curves); non-engineered classes (adobe, unreinforced masonry, timber) get a heuristic 35% damage reduction. Costs are a percentage of replacement value by material (MUR 30%, CR 20%, W/S 15%, default 25%). These are screening assumptions for TA targeting, not engineering estimates.

Key assumptions & caveats

  • The single benefit-cost ratio counts all three resilience dividends: avoided direct losses + avoided-reconstruction carbon (D1), the GDP stimulus of the program's spending (D2 — note this is a multiplier on the same spending that forms the cost side), and the durability of compliant construction (D3). Lives and DALYs are never monetised — no value is placed on life in this analysis; they are reported as counts.
  • Jobs preserved are the avoided losses re-expressed in employment units (avoided losses ÷ GDP per labor hour ÷ 2,000 hours/yr) — they are never added to the BCR as dollars, which would double-count the same losses.
  • The construction premium is priced as a percentage of each location's unit construction cost (default 3% for a one-level upgrade; sensitivity 1–12% in the notebook) and applies to all complying new floor area regardless of local hazard — country-wide BCRs are lower than hazard-targeted enforcement.
  • Compliance is scaled by a building practice factor (WGI + subnational corruption + education, after Daniell et al. 2014).
  • The existing stock turns over at memoryless per-cohort rates (oldest cohort 1.5%/yr → newest 0.5%/yr; roughly two-thirds of today's stock survives to 2075); replaced buildings are rebuilt to the prevailing code and pay the premium. Age-based replacement is infeasible — GHSL records no construction year before 1975 — so age-free exponential rates are used, with code-level cohorts as the age proxy. Freezing the stock (rates = 0) would be more conservative: replacements would never adopt the new code.
  • Hazard: GEM PGA 475yr; other return periods by piecewise log-log scaling (steeper below the anchor — slope 0.50/0.33), benchmarked against GEM and GIRI country AAL references.
  • All five SSPs are identical in 2025 and diverge after.
