About this demo

A cost-benefit explorer for seismic building-code adoption and enforcement. For each country it models today's building stock on a 1 km grid (floor areas × unit construction costs × GEM building typologies × construction-vintage code levels), pairs it with GEM vulnerability functions and the GEM seismic hazard map, and runs a cost-benefit analysis of raising new construction to the next code level — across all five SSP growth scenarios, over a 25-, 50- or 75-year horizon (2050 / 2075 / 2100; 50 years is the default everywhere).

What the map shows

Everything is aggregated onto H3 hexagons (size auto-scaled to the country; roughly 36–253 km² per hex). Values are summed per hex and ratios derived after aggregation. The mosaic covers the country's whole land area — hexes with no modelled built-up area show as zero/no-data tiles rather than holes; ocean and large lakes are excluded. Map views:

  • Seismic hazard — mean peak ground acceleration with a 475-year return period (GEM Global Seismic Hazard Map).
  • Exposure 2025 — building replacement value per hex.
  • Growth — new floor area added between 2025 and the selected horizon (SSP2 forecast).
  • Risk metrics:
    • BCR — total benefits of all three resilience dividends ÷ discounted costs (SSP2) — the same ratio as the tiles and the chart. National program costs (and their stimulus) are allocated to hexes by their share of discounted new-construction value.
    • Total benefits — NPV of all three dividends (SSP2).
    • Annual loss — baseline 2025 average annual earthquake loss (USD/yr).
    • Loss rate — the average annual earthquake loss expressed as a share of the building stock's replacement value (e.g. 0.2% means yearly expected losses equal to 0.2% of what the buildings would cost to rebuild).

Triple dividend of resilience

Benefits are grouped under the Triple Dividend of Resilience framework (Tanner et al., ODI/GFDRR/World Bank), with each dollar counted exactly once:

  • 1st dividend — avoided losses when disasters strike: the headline avoided structural + contents losses (direct damage only), plus the embodied carbon of avoided reconstruction — carbon intensity from GEM's carbon accounting (CARBON_BUILDINGS_TON in the exposure model), valued at the World Bank shadow price of carbon. Lives saved and DALYs averted are annotated as counts.
  • 2nd dividend — unlocked economic potential: the GDP stimulus of the code program's spending (public-investment multipliers — IMF 2014 0.25/0.5, Adarov 2024/Ardanaz 2025 1.1 reference, Barbero 2024 RRF 1.22; multiplier channel only), with jobs preserved and created annotated as counts.
  • 3rd dividend — co-benefits of the investment itself: durability — code-compliant construction lasts longer, so the annualized cost of premature replacement falls. Conservatively scoped: service life 50→55 years, accruing only on the structural share (~30%) of compliant new value — the premium buys a better structural system, so the longevity claim excludes finishes and services (≈ 0.05%/yr of building value).

There is exactly one benefit-cost ratio on this site: total benefits of all three dividends ÷ costs, identical in the summary tiles, the cumulative chart, and every hex cell on the map (at the selected horizon). Investment costs are broken down into government policy development (upfront), government enforcement & permitting (ongoing), and the public's higher construction costs (the premium). DALYs are estimated from fatalities (GEM publishes no injury functions): deaths × years of life lost (WB life expectancy) plus an injury ratio — parameters stated in the notebook outputs. Context statistics cite EM-DAT (CRED/UCLouvain), World Bank life expectancy (for the DALY comparison) and an assumed 40-year working career (for the jobs comparison — the World Bank publishes no expected-working-life indicator).

Scenario toggles on the chart

The chart's discount-rate and construction-premium toggles recompute costs and benefits client-side from the exported undiscounted flows — both are linear, so the recombination is exact. The summary tiles, dividends panel and map keep the headline assumptions (5% discount, the country's default premium). Building-practice-factor sensitivity is not client-computable (it acts nonlinearly through the vulnerability-curve mixing) and lives in the notebook instead.

Regulatory profile & compliance (Building Regulations Atlas)

Each country page links to the GFDRR Building Regulations Atlas and uses its indicators in two scoped ways:

  • Structural-design packages — only the Atlas's Building Design → Structural Resilience indicators. Evaluating individual code provisions is beyond this analysis; instead they are grouped into the same code-level ladder the vulnerability model uses, consistent with Crowley et al. (2021), where levels reflect the sophistication of lateral-force design requirements: basic seismic coefficients (low), dynamic analysis + site effects + drift control (moderate), capacity design + ductile detailing (high). The suggested package for a level jump is indicative — not exhaustive, items are not individually costed, and not every item contributes quantified benefits.
  • Compliance score — all binary Compliance Mechanisms indicators reduced to one factor per country with a rank-1 alternating-least-squares factor model fitted on observed entries only (a PCA analogue suited to binary items). This handles the Atlas's uneven coverage — some indicators come from regional reports (e.g. Sub-Saharan Africa) and simply don't exist for other countries — because loadings are shared globally and each country is scored from whatever subset it has. The score enters the building practice factor as one weighted component (alongside governance, education and corruption measures). The practice factor is the model's estimate of the share of new construction actually built to code — the site headlines that percentage; the Atlas score is a proxy input to it, not a compliance rate itself.
  • Compliance-improvement packages — groups of mechanisms (plan review, construction-phase inspections, professional qualification, transparency/digital permitting). Closing a package's gaps raises the compliance score, hence the practice factor, hence the protection actually delivered. Benefits and premium costs both scale roughly linearly with the practice factor, so packages raise total protection while the BCR itself barely moves — impacts are shown as a proportional benefit uplift, not an engine re-run.

Retrofit priorities

Code changes only reach new construction. The retrofit table ranks the existing stock's building classes by avoided AAL if retrofitted: engineered classes move one code level up (GEM curves); non-engineered classes (adobe, unreinforced masonry, timber) get a heuristic 35% damage reduction. Costs are a percentage of the structural share of replacement value by material (MUR 30%, CR 20%, W/S 15%, default 25%), and benefits use the same triple-dividend accounting as the headline BCR (avoided losses + avoided-reconstruction carbon, plus the stimulus of the retrofit spend; no durability claim). These are screening assumptions for TA targeting, not engineering estimates.

Key assumptions & caveats

  • The single benefit-cost ratio counts all three resilience dividends: avoided direct losses + avoided-reconstruction carbon (D1), the GDP stimulus of the program's spending (D2 — note this is a multiplier on the same spending that forms the cost side), and the durability of compliant construction (D3). Lives and DALYs are never monetised — no value is placed on life in this analysis; they are reported as counts.
  • Jobs preserved are the avoided losses re-expressed in employment units (avoided losses ÷ GDP per labor hour ÷ 2,000 hours/yr) — they are never added to the BCR as dollars, which would double-count the same losses.
  • The construction premium is priced as a percentage of the structural share of each location's unit construction cost (roughly 30% of the building cost — a stated HAZUS-order assumption, as GEM's structural-cost field is unpopulated; contents never bear a premium). Default 3% for a one-level upgrade; the chart's premium toggle spans 1–12%. The premium applies to all complying new floor area regardless of local hazard — country-wide BCRs are lower than hazard-targeted enforcement.
  • Compliance is scaled by a building practice factor (WGI + subnational corruption + education, after Daniell et al. 2014).
  • The existing stock's code-level mix blends GHSL built-up vintage (the spatial pattern of older vs newer stock) with GEM's performance-based level shares per typology — so pre-code buildings that perform like low-code designs are credited, and post-adoption construction is not assumed fully compliant.
  • The existing stock turns over at memoryless per-cohort rates (oldest cohort 1.5%/yr → newest 0.5%/yr; roughly two-thirds of today's stock survives to 2075); replaced buildings are rebuilt to the prevailing code and pay the premium. Age-based replacement is infeasible — GHSL records no construction year before 1975 — so age-free exponential rates are used, with code-level cohorts as the age proxy. Freezing the stock (rates = 0) would be more conservative: replacements would never adopt the new code.
  • Hazard: GEM PGA 475yr; other return periods by piecewise log-log scaling (steeper below the anchor — slope 0.50/0.33), benchmarked against GEM and GIRI country AAL references.
  • All five SSPs are identical in 2025 and diverge after.
